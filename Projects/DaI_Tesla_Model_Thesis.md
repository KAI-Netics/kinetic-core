## The New Vertical: Drone-as-Infrastructure (DaI)

**Positioning statement:**
> "We don't sell drone flights. We build drone infrastructure — and then operate it, staff it, and license the intelligence layer on top."

### Three layers of DaI:

**Physical layer (FP)**
- DFR pads = Supercharger stations
- Resiliency hubs at hospitals, campuses, government facilities
- Whoever owns the pad owns the customer relationship
- Revenue: pad installation, operations contracts, per-flight fees

**Human layer (EAF)**
- CAKO graduates = captive operator supply chain
- Every certified pilot is a potential FP operator or CURN node
- Grant-funded training = Gigafactory logic with OPM (other people's money)
- Revenue: training fees, workforce contracts, EARN/FEMA grants

**Software layer (MONK)**
- AKMS + Core Engine = FSD equivalent
- Licensable to other operators, municipalities, federal agencies
- Flight telemetry data as an asset (route optimization, safety analytics)
- DHS approved C-UAS tech list candidate
- Revenue: licensing fees, SBIR awards, data subscriptions

## Jevons Paradox Layer — Added April 11, 2026
Source: Farzad, "Tesla Just Made the Biggest Bet in Industrial History" (YouTube, Feb 27, 2026)

### What Jevons Paradox Says

When a resource becomes dramatically cheaper through efficiency gains, *total consumption increases* rather than decreasing. The efficiency doesn't conserve demand — it *expands* it by unlocking use cases that were previously uneconomical.

Tesla Robotaxi thesis: drop cost-per-mile to $0.20 and entirely new trip categories emerge — elderly mobility, spontaneous errands, underserved communities — that didn't exist at $2.00/mile.

### The Drone Jevons Paradox for KAI-Netics

**Current state:** Drone delivery is expensive. Use cases are limited to high-value, time-critical items (critical specimens, high-cost pharmaceuticals) where the cost-per-flight is justified.

**Jevons trigger:** When DFR pad infrastructure is in place and CAKO operators run scheduled routes, cost-per-flight drops to the point where *new trip categories* become economical:
- Routine pharmacy courier runs
- Biohazard specimen pickup
- Cafeteria and internal supply runs
- Last-mile government/municipal logistics
- Administrative document delivery
- Disaster resiliency logistics
- Same-day healthcare transport for underserved populations

None of these trips exist at $150/flight. All of them become viable at $8-15/flight at scale.

### Four Things to Adopt from the Jevons/Robotaxi Model

**1. Cost-per-flight as north star metric**
- Tesla's entire thesis pivots on $0.20/mile
- KAI-Netics needs its equivalent number
- Model: what is current FP cost-per-flight? What is the target at 10-pad deployment scale?
- That delta IS the Jevons story for investors, grant committees, and anchor customers

**2. Infrastructure density creates demand — not the other way around**
- You cannot demonstrate Jevons expansion without pad density making low-cost economics real
- MEIA, FEMA, CURN, and DFR pad investment are all infrastructure-density arguments
- Frame every grant application around unlocking the Jevons demand curve

**3. Map the "new trip" categories explicitly**
- These are the Jevons expansion opportunities — trips that don't economically exist today
- Each new trip category = a new revenue line in the pro forma
- Build a table: trip type | current cost barrier | viable at | annual volume estimate | revenue potential

**4. MONK as the flywheel accelerant**
- Every FP flight generates data → MONK IP becomes more valuable
- More valuable MONK → more licensing revenue → funds more infrastructure
- More infrastructure → lower cost-per-flight → Jevons demand expansion → more flights → more data
- This is the same flywheel Tesla is betting its future on with FSD

### The Jevons Pitch Statement (draft)
> "Today's drone delivery market is like the Model T era — real technology, but priced for early adopters only. KAI-Netics is building the infrastructure layer that drives cost-per-flight below the Jevons threshold — the point where entirely new categories of demand become economical. When that happens, we don't just own a drone company. We own the infrastructure for a transportation category that didn't previously exist."
